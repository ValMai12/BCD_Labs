The roof of Casa Batlló

The roof of Casa Batlló, one of Antoni Gaudí’s most celebrated masterpieces in Barcelona, is a striking and imaginative feature that exemplifies his genius in blending architecture with natural forms and symbolism. Often described as one of the most emblematic elements of the building, the roof immediately captures attention with its dynamic, almost fantastical appearance, which has invited countless interpretations over the years. Perhaps the most famous interpretation is that the roof resembles the back of a fearsome dragon, a nod to the legend of Saint George, Catalonia’s patron saint, and his legendary battle with a dragon. This reference is not just superficial; Gaudí’s design subtly incorporates the story into the architecture itself. The roof is covered with large, overlapping ceramic tiles that glisten in sunlight, mimicking the iridescent scales of a dragon. These tiles, carefully arranged in gradients of colors ranging from deep reds to shimmering greens and blues, give the roof a sense of movement and life, as if the dragon itself is undulating across the top of the building. Structurally, the roof sits atop a series of double mansards, a traditional roofing element that Gaudí adapted and transformed to suit his organic vision. Rather than following rigid geometrical rules, the roof’s forms curve and twist, echoing the natural world in the flowing shapes of waves, hills, and animal forms. The design demonstrates Gaudí’s philosophy of “organic architecture,” where buildings grow naturally from their environment and take on forms inspired by nature.
